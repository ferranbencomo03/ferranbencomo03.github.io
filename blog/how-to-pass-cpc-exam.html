How to pass the CPC medical coding exam

A practical study plan for the AAPC Certified Professional Coder exam.

The CPC exam from the AAPC is one of the most recognized credentials in medical coding — and it's very passable once you know how it's built and how to work fast with your code books. This guide breaks the exam down and gives you a 10-step plan.

What the CPC exam actually tests

The CPC is an open-book exam (you bring your approved AMA CPT, ICD-10-CM and HCPCS Level II code books) with around 100 multiple-choice questions and roughly 4 hours to finish. Always confirm the current format on the AAPC website, as details change. The questions span:

  • Medical terminology and anatomy
  • ICD-10-CM diagnosis coding (the "why")
  • CPT and HCPCS Level II procedure coding (the "what")
  • Modifiers, coding guidelines and bundling (NCCI)
  • Compliance, reimbursement and practice management

The single biggest skill: working your code books fast

Because it's open-book, the candidates who pass aren't the ones who memorize the most — they're the ones who navigate the books quickly. Two habits matter most:

  • Index first, then verify. Look up the main term in the Index, then always confirm the code in the Tabular/section before you choose it. Never code straight from the Index.
  • Tab and highlight your books before exam day — section guidelines, the modifier list, common tables. Seconds saved per question add up over 100 questions.

A 10-step study plan

  1. Learn the structure of each code set before any codes (how CPT's 6 sections and ICD-10-CM are organized).
  2. Master medical terminology by word parts (prefix + root + suffix) so any term is decodable.
  3. Drill ICD-10-CM conventions: Excludes1 vs Excludes2, "code first", 7th characters, laterality, sequencing.
  4. Learn E/M coding the current way — office visits are leveled by Medical Decision Making (MDM) or time.
  5. Memorize the most-tested modifiers (25, 59, 51, 50, 26/TC, 76/77, 57…) and when each applies.
  6. Understand compliance: fraud vs abuse, medical necessity, NCCI edits, the global surgical package.
  7. Practice timed — bank the quick questions, flag long surgical scenarios, and return to them.
  8. Take full-length practice exams and review every wrong answer until you know why.
  9. Keep a one-page cheat sheet of the rules you keep forgetting, and review it daily.
  10. The week before: tab your books, sleep, and trust your process.
