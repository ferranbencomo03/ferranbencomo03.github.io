How to study for the NCLEX: high-yield topics & tips

Study smarter, not longer — for nursing students preparing for the NCLEX.

The NCLEX doesn't test whether you memorized every fact in nursing school. It tests whether you can make safe clinical judgments and prioritize. Once you study with that in mind, the whole exam gets easier. Here's where to focus.

Think like the NCLEX

  • Safety and prioritization first. When a question gives you four "correct" actions, choose the one that keeps the patient safest right now. Remember ABCs (Airway, Breathing, Circulation) and Maslow.
  • Assess before you act — unless the situation is an obvious emergency.
  • The nurse's scope. Many answers come down to what a nurse should do first, teach, or delegate.

High-yield topics worth your time

You can't study everything equally. These areas show up again and again:

  • Pharmacology — drug classes, suffixes, key side effects and nursing considerations (this is where many students lose points).
  • Lab values — the normal ranges and what an abnormal value means for your patient.
  • Fluids & electrolytes — sodium, potassium, calcium imbalances and their signs.
  • Prioritization & delegation — who to see first, what to delegate to a UAP or LPN.
  • Infection control, safety, and common complications.

A simple study routine

  1. Do practice questions daily — and read the rationale for every answer, right or wrong.
  2. Turn weak areas into one-page cheat sheets you review each morning.
  3. Study actively: explain concepts out loud, draw them, quiz a study buddy.
  4. Use spaced repetition — revisit a topic after 1 day, 3 days, then a week.
  5. Protect your sleep the week of the exam; a rested brain recalls more.
